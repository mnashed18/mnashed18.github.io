Introduction

Many people who watch basketball complain that the NBA has become soft because there is a surfeit of foul calls throughout the game. Social media has heightened how controversial the officiating has become in the NBA. Some people have described the NBA as unwatchable because of how many foul calls are made, lengthening the game beyond what it is supposed to be. Players now flop excessively in order to get a foul call, and it appears to work in the players favor. Flop refers to excessively acting like you were hit by an opposing player. Additionally, when a foul is not called, players will whine and complain to the referee because they are used to getting foul calls in their favor. However, on ESPN, a sports television show, former NBA player JJ Reddick stated that free throws are currently not occurring as much during NBA basketball games. I want to utilize the data provided by Basketball Reference to determine whether foul calls are now more common or in excess than they were over a decade ago. I hypothesize that the foul calls are actually not being called as often and that the type of fouls that are being called are different.

Some questions I want to answer are: Are fouls more prevalent now then over ten years ago? Are there more free throws occurring due to fouls? Are fouls resulting in more points? These questions are important to answer because they are crucial in the discussion of whether the league is softer. People cannot complain about the officiating if foul calls and free throw attempts are decreasing.

TtestResult(statistic=-11.976255111920844, pvalue=7.484713822453521e-24, df=156.0)

Here I am conducting a T Test on two dataframes I created. One dataframe named old_df represents how the NBA was officiated over a decade ago, which is marked by having a year that is less than 2014. The new_df dataframe represents the how the NBA is officiated in today's era, which is the decade 2014-2024. The column I decide to conduct the T Test on is the personal foul column, which represents the average number of personal fouls that are called during the game. This will help determine if there is a significant difference in between how many fouls were called over a decade ago and now. The p-value obtained from the t-test is far less than 0.05, which means that the result is statistically significant.

In [ ]:
model = linear_model.LinearRegression()
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error, r2_score
model.fit(X_train, Y_train)
Y_pred = model.predict(X_test)
plt.scatter(X, Y)
plt.plot(X_test, Y_pred, color='blue', linewidth=3)
plt.title("Personal Fouls Per Game versus Year")
plt.xlabel("Year")
plt.ylabel("Average Personal Fouls")
plt.show()
print('Mean squared error: %.2f' % mean_squared_error(Y_test, Y_pred))
print('R2 Score: %.2f'% r2_score(Y_test, Y_pred))

Mean squared error: 2.78
R2 Score: 0.70

The R2 score being relatively high means there is some linear correlation between the years and the amount of foul calls. Additionally, the Mean squared error is not too high, especially when understanding how many foul calls are being made every game. The foul calls are overall decreasing as years go by, which is indicated by the line that shows the trend. The data points represent the average personal fouls called during a year by a specific referee. The model is a good fit and supports the hypothesis that foul calls are decreasing and the league has not grown soft. The R2 Score being closer to 1 indicates that the model is a good fit because the test data is being predicted at a high clip

Insights and Conclusions

Conclusions that we can draw is that over the years, both foul calls and free throw attempts are decreasing as the years go by. The argument that the game is being made longer by foul calls is not supported by the data or the models because if there are less free throws occurring, then less time is being wasted on foul calls. Additionally, personal fouls are linearly decreasing, which can be visualized by a linear regression.

If I were to conduct further research on the topic, I would compare the length (time) of games as the years go by to see if the games are actually taking too long. I would also take a larger sample size of refs in order to truly grasp the magnitude at which fouls are decreasing.

Walking away from the research that now has been conducted, we can now say that it is unfair to criticize the National Basketball Association and describe the league as "soft" because of an excess of fouls. The fouls are decreasing so an excess does not even exist. A possible reason I propose for why there are so many complaints about the softness of the league is that social media has put a spotlight on every call being made by referees. Referees are on the hot seat every day to perform. Every call they mess up is being recorded and being put on Instagram for everyone, including myself, to see.
